Civic Cube: Theoretical Foundations and Methodological Framework of a Three-Dimensional Political Compass

Civic Nexus Research
2025
Foundational Paper — Series 1

Abstract

Abstract

This paper presents the theoretical foundations and methodological design of the Civic Cube Political Compass, a three-dimensional ideological measurement instrument developed by Civic Nexus. Existing two-dimensional compass models systematically fail to capture the moral and cultural dimensions of political identity, reducing complex ideological structures to an impoverished left-right and authoritarian-libertarian plane. The Civic Cube addresses this limitation by introducing a third axis, Adherence, which measures the degree to which an individual holds principled versus relativistic ethical commitments. Drawing on historical documents, classical and contemporary political philosophy, and the primary writings of ideological traditions, this paper defines each axis, documents the philosophical grounding of each of the eight resulting ideological positions, and describes the instrument's sixty-question design across six thematic subsections. The model is intended as a philosophically rigorous alternative to entertainment-oriented compass tools, grounded in the actual historical and philosophical meaning of political terms rather than their contemporary popular usage.

Background

Political self-identification is one of the most consequential and poorly understood aspects of civic life. The proliferation of online political compass tests over the past two decades has created widespread public engagement with ideological self-placement, yet the dominant instruments in this space were designed primarily for accessibility and entertainment rather than analytical rigour. The result is a landscape in which millions of people hold strong views about their own political identity that may rest on definitional foundations that bear little relationship to the historical or philosophical meaning of the labels they have adopted.

The most widely used instrument, the Political Compass test, reduces political identity to two axes: economic (left to right) and social (libertarian to authoritarian). While this simplification has proven pedagogically useful, it produces systematic misclassifications that have become well-documented in academic commentary. Most notably, it fails to distinguish between different sources and justifications for authority, collapses morally distinct positions into the same quadrant, and is broadly acknowledged to reflect contemporary Western ideological assumptions rather than historically grounded definitions.

Limitations of Existing Two-Dimensional Models

The central limitation of two-dimensional political mapping is that it cannot distinguish between individuals who hold the same economic and authority positions for fundamentally different philosophical reasons. A committed Burkean conservative and a corporatist may occupy identical coordinates on a standard two-axis compass despite holding radically different moral frameworks, conceptions of community, and relationships to state authority. Similarly, a corporatist and an anarcho-capitalist may appear in the same quadrant despite disagreeing on nearly every substantive question of property and collective organization.

This conflation arises because two-dimensional models treat moral commitment, the degree to which an individual adheres to a principled, universal ethical code versus a contextual, pragmatic, or relativistic one, as a secondary feature of political identity rather than a constitutive dimension. Historically, the distinction between principled and relativistic ethical frameworks has been one of the most significant fault lines within political ideologies. It separates, for example, natural-rights libertarianism from pragmatic liberalism, orthodox Marxism from revisionist social democracy, and religious conservatism from secular traditionalism. A measurement instrument that cannot capture this distinction is structurally incapable of accurately placing a significant portion of the ideological spectrum.

A further limitation of existing models is their reliance on contemporary popular usage of political terminology rather than historically grounded definitions. Terms such as "fascism," "socialism," "libertarianism," and "anarchism" have acquired contested and often contradictory meanings in public discourse. An instrument that defines its axes by reference to current popular usage will produce results that shift with cultural fashion rather than reflecting durable ideological structures. The Civic Cube is designed to address this by grounding all definitions in primary sources, the foundational philosophical and historical documents of each tradition, rather than in how those terms are currently deployed in popular media.

Theoretical Framework

The Civic Cube model defines political identity as a position in a three-dimensional space, with each dimension corresponding to a fundamental question about the organization of society and the relationship between the individual and the collective. Each axis is scored on a normalized scale from −1 to +1, with the sign and magnitude of each score jointly determining an individual's ideological classification.

Axis I Economic Axis
− Socialist ←————————→ + Capitalist

The Economic axis measures an individual's position on questions of ownership, production, and the distribution of material resources. It is anchored on the left by collectivist ownership, the belief that productive resources, key institutions, and economic systems should be owned and governed by groups rather than individuals, and on the right by private ownership, the belief that individuals should retain exclusive control over property, capital, and the fruits of their labor.

This axis draws on the foundational distinction in Western political economy between the traditions represented by Marx and Engels, who argued in The Communist Manifesto (1848) that "the theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence: Abolition of private property", and those represented by Adam Smith, whose Wealth of Nations (1776) established the philosophical basis for market-based private ownership as the engine of prosperity and the natural condition of free individuals. Milton Friedman's Capitalism and Freedom (1962) provides the modern articulation of the capitalist position, arguing that economic and political freedom are inseparable and that central control of economic life necessarily threatens civil liberty. The socialist pole draws on the tradition of collective ownership as an instrument of genuine democratic self-governance rather than mere efficiency, a distinction developed extensively in the market socialism debate documented by Rees (1993).

Axis II Authority Axis
− Reformist ←————————→ + Traditionalist

The Authority axis measures an individual's relationship to the legitimacy, scope, and necessity of coercive institutional power. The positive (Traditionalist) pole reflects the view that strong, centralised authority is a necessary and legitimate feature of social organization, that without a sovereign capable of enforcing order, social life degrades into conflict. The negative (Reformist) pole reflects scepticism toward or outright rejection of coercive hierarchies, holding that authority must be radically limited, continuously challenged, or abolished entirely.

The philosophical grounding of the Traditionalist pole draws primarily on Thomas Hobbes's Leviathan (1651), which argues that in the absence of an absolute sovereign, the natural condition of humanity is a "war of all against all" in which life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Hobbes provides the most thoroughgoing philosophical case for centralized authority as a prerequisite for any stable social order. The fascist tradition, as articulated by Giovanni Gentile, philosopher and primary author of the Doctrine of Fascism (1932), extends this toward a total identification of the individual with the state, in which authority is not merely necessary but ethically constitutive: the state as the realisation of ethical will. This philosophical position is distinct from mere authoritarianism and is essential to accurately locating fascist ideology. The Reformist pole draws on John Locke's Two Treatises of Government (1689), which grounds the legitimacy of authority entirely in the consent of the governed and establishes natural rights as a limit on sovereign power, and on the anarchist tradition as developed by Todd May in The Political Philosophy of Post-structuralist Anarchism (1994), which holds that all hierarchies of authority are philosophically unjustifiable and should be dissolved rather than reformed.

Axis III Adherence Axis
− Relativistic ←————————→ + Principled

The Adherence axis is the Civic Cube's most distinctive contribution to political measurement. It captures the degree to which an individual holds that moral and social norms are universal, fixed, and binding, as opposed to contextual, constructed, and adaptable. This dimension is entirely absent from two-dimensional models, yet it is one of the most historically significant fault lines within political traditions.

The Principled pole reflects the view that there exist objective or transcendent moral standards, whether grounded in natural law, divine revelation, cultural tradition, or rational philosophy, to which individuals and societies are obligated to conform. Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics provides the foundational philosophical statement of virtue as a stable, cultivated character trait embedded in community life. Robert Putnam's Bowling Alone (2000) documents the social consequences of the erosion of shared norms, providing an empirical grounding for the principled position's claim that community coherence depends on stable moral commitments. The Relativistic pole holds that moral and social norms are historically contingent, culturally determined, and should evolve with circumstances and individual expression. Friedrich Nietzsche's Beyond Good and Evil (1886) represents the most radical philosophical articulation of this position, challenging the legitimacy of all inherited moral systems. David Riesman's The Lonely Crowd (1950) provides a sociological analysis of the shift from "inner-directed" individuals governed by internalized principles to "other-directed" individuals governed by social cues and peer expectations, a distinction that closely maps to the Adherence axis.

Ideological Classification

The combination of sign values across the three axes produces eight ideological octants, each corresponding to a named political tradition. Classification is determined by the sign of each normalized axis score: a positive score on a given axis indicates orientation toward the right/traditionalist/principled pole; a negative score indicates orientation toward the left/reformist/relativistic pole. The magnitude of each score indicates the strength of that orientation, respondents near the origin on any axis are genuinely centrist on that dimension, while those at the extremes hold strong, consistent positions.

The ideology labels used by the Civic Cube are grounded in the historical and philosophical definitions of each tradition, not in contemporary popular usage. The placement of ideologies is the result of examining the core philosophical commitments of each tradition across the three axes, not an attempt to locate contemporary political parties or figures, which inevitably introduces confounding from short-term policy positions.

A key case worth addressing explicitly is the placement of historical fascism. On the standard two-dimensional Political Compass, fascism is typically placed in the far-right authoritarian quadrant, a placement driven primarily by its association with nationalist cultural politics and its historical opposition to communist movements. However, this placement obscures the economic and moral structure of fascist ideology. Gentile's doctrine describes the state as the total ethical community within which individual identity is subsumed, a position that is collectivist in its rejection of autonomous private interest, authoritarian in its absolute concentration of power, and principled in its demand for total moral conformity to the national will. On the Civic Cube, this maps to the Realist Socialist octant: collectivist economics, high authority, and high adherence. This is not a relativisation of fascism's historical crimes, it is a structurally more accurate account of its philosophical commitments.

Methodology

The Civic Cube instrument consists of 60 statements distributed evenly across six thematic subsections, with 10 statements per subsection. The six subsections are organized into three pairs, each pair corresponding to one of the three axes. Respondents rate each statement on a four-point scale: Agree, Somewhat Agree, Somewhat Disagree, or Disagree.

Question Design Principles

Questions were designed with three principles in mind. First, statements address policy-level positions rather than value abstractions, the intent is to measure what respondents believe should happen, not merely what they value in the abstract. Abstract value questions (e.g., "Do you value freedom?") produce near-universal agreement that reveals nothing about ideological differentiation. Second, statements are drawn from genuine points of historical and contemporary political dispute, areas where different ideological traditions have taken clearly divergent positions grounded in their foundational commitments. Third, statements are constructed to avoid framing effects that advantage one pole over the other, neither the positive nor the negative response should be obviously "correct" to a neutral reader.

The thematic sections and their ideological functions are as follows:

Section 1.1 — Property & Production

Measures views on the ownership of productive resources, the legitimacy of private capital accumulation, and the appropriate relationship between individuals and the means of production. Draws on the classical property rights tradition (Locke, Smith) and the Marxist critique of private appropriation (Marx, Engels). Contributes to the Economic axis score.

Section 1.2 — Wealth & Redistribution

Measures views on the legitimacy of economic inequality, the role of taxation and redistribution, and the moral obligations of wealth. Contributes to the Economic axis score alongside Section 1.1, with particular sensitivity to market socialist versus laissez-faire positions.

Section 2.1 — Law & Order

Measures views on the legitimacy and appropriate scope of state coercive power in maintaining social order, criminal justice, policing, civil law, and the balance between security and liberty. Draws on Hobbesian arguments for sovereign authority and Lockean arguments for natural rights as limits on state power. Contributes to the Authority axis score.

Section 2.2 — Power & Rebellion

Measures views on political authority, the legitimacy of dissent and revolution, and the conditions under which existing power structures may or should be challenged. Draws on Gentile's doctrine of the state as ethical totality, on Locke's right of revolution, and on anarchist arguments for the dissolution of coercive hierarchy. Contributes to the Authority axis score.

Section 3.1 — Faith & Morality

Measures views on the role of religious or transcendent moral frameworks in public and political life, the relationship between secular governance and moral tradition, and the appropriate weight given to religious communities in shaping social norms. Contributes to the Adherence axis score, with high-adherence respondents typically affirming the public relevance of moral tradition and low-adherence respondents favouring secular pluralism.

Section 3.2 — Tradition & Progress

Measures views on cultural continuity, the pace and direction of social change, and the value of inherited institutions and practices. Draws on Burke's argument that society is a "partnership between the dead, the living, and the unborn" and on progressive traditions that treat historical change as normatively desirable. Contributes to the Adherence axis score alongside Section 3.1.

Scoring and Normalization

Each response is scored on a weighted scale: Agree = +1, Somewhat Agree = +0.5, Somewhat Disagree = −0.5, Disagree = −1. The raw score for each axis is calculated as the mean of all weighted responses across the two subsections constituting that axis, ten questions per subsection, twenty per axis, yielding a raw axis score in the range [−1, +1].

Axis score formula: For each axis, the score S is calculated as S = (Σwᵢ) / n, where wᵢ is the weighted response to question i and n = 20. The resulting score S ∈ [−1, +1]. Ideology classification is assigned by the vector of signs (sgn(S_economic), sgn(S_authority), sgn(S_adherence)), producing one of eight named octants.

Respondents whose score on any axis falls within a predefined band around zero (|S| < 0.15) are flagged as genuinely centrist on that dimension. These respondents are classified by their two stronger axes with a centrist qualifier on the third, for example, a respondent who scores strongly socialist and reformist but nearly zero on adherence would be classified as a centrist corporatist rather than being forced into a binary classification that misrepresents their actual position.

All data submitted to the Civic Nexus backend is anonymised before storage. No personally identifiable information is retained. The backend stores axis scores and ideology labels only, alongside optional voluntarily-submitted demographic fields. Respondents who do not provide demographic information are included in all axis-level analyses.

Discussion

The Civic Cube represents a deliberate methodological departure from the dominant approach to political compass design. Where most instruments prioritize accessibility and immediate interpretability, the Civic Cube prioritizes definitional accuracy, accepting that some positions will be counter-intuitive to users whose understanding of political terms has been shaped by popular usage rather than historical or philosophical grounding.

The most significant departure is the treatment of fascism. By classifying historical fascism as a form of realist socialism, on the basis of its collectivist economic structure, its absolute identification of the individual with the state, and its demand for total moral conformity to a fixed national doctrine, the Civic Cube produces a result that contradicts the near-universal convention of placing fascism on the economic right. This is not a political claim about the relative merits of right and left politics; it is a structural claim about the philosophical commitments of the fascist tradition as articulated in its own foundational documents, particularly Gentile's Doctrine of Fascism. The standard two-dimensional model's placement of fascism on the right derives largely from its cultural and nationalist politics and its historical conflict with communist movements, neither of which speaks to the question of economic ownership or the relationship between individual and state authority.

A further methodological commitment is the refusal to define ideological positions by reference to contemporary political parties or figures. Modern political parties are coalitions of often-incompatible philosophical traditions, and their policy positions shift continuously with electoral pressures. An ideological compass grounded in party positions will necessarily be unstable, geographically parochial, and quickly dated. The Civic Cube instead locates positions by reference to the philosophical traditions from which they emerged, traditions that are substantially more stable across time and geography.

The primary limitation of the current instrument is the inherent difficulty of constructing questions that are genuinely neutral with respect to ideological framing. Despite systematic effort to avoid framing effects, any natural-language instrument will contain residual biases that are difficult to eliminate entirely. Future development will include formal inter-rater reliability testing of individual questions and ongoing review of the question bank as the dataset grows. The centrist band threshold (|S| < 0.15) is also provisional and subject to revision based on empirical analysis of the score distribution.
